CASE STUDY · COIN MASTER · MOON ACTIVE
Progression · Economy · Content · Tooling
OVERVIEW

Wildland Adventure is an exploration and progression feature inside Coin Master. Players collect Potions, clear obstacles, earn Flags, fill the Stage progress bar, receive rewards, and advance through a structured path.

As the first Product Level Designer dedicated to the feature, I designed high-level Stage layouts and translated Economy and Product direction into executable progression experiences, while coordinating content production, implementation, testing, and iteration.

>
CHALLENGE

Wildland introduced a fundamentally different gameplay pattern into Coin Master - shifting players from the familiar spin-driven core loop into a spatial exploration experience based on resource spending, obstacle clearing, progression, and Stage completion.

The challenge was to make this new interaction model immediately understandable within a mature live game, while ensuring it complemented the wider product ecosystem rather than competing with it for player attention, time, or resources. At the same time, obstacle costs, resource distribution, rewards, player momentum, and a continuous LiveOps production schedule had to remain balanced - layout, Economy, pacing, and production constraints working together as one connected system.

APPROACH

I translated Product and Economy direction into playable Chapter layouts, progression configurations, content requirements, and production-ready solutions.

  • ·High-level Chapter and Stage layouts, route structure, and area sequencing.
  • ·Obstacle, resource, and reward placement tuned for progression pacing.
  • ·When tests showed a mid-Stage bottleneck, I redistributed cost, obstacle density, and reward cadence with Economy - preserving the total cost envelope while improving momentum toward completion.
  • ·Chapter Dressing, art and asset requirements, and internal tooling needs.
  • ·Use cases and acceptance criteria, iteration, validation, and QA support.

OUTCOME
  • ·Contributed to a repeatable production process for live Stage and Chapter creation.

REFLECTION
What Wildland Taught Me About Systems

Wildland strengthened my understanding that progression design is not a sequence of isolated stages, but a connected product system.

Each layout decision could affect several dimensions at once: resource consumption, reward timing, perceived progress, pacing, and the player's understanding of what to pursue next. A stage could work locally while still creating problems across the wider journey.

This pushed me to evaluate content at multiple levels: the immediate player experience, the chapter and stage structure, the economy requirements, and the production system needed to support ongoing delivery.

It also changed how I approach product work more broadly. I became more deliberate about identifying dependencies early, separating local optimization from system-level impact, and designing solutions that remain understandable, scalable, and executable across teams.

CASE STUDY · COIN MASTER · MOON ACTIVE
Coin Master · Moon ActiveProduct Level DesignerNon-Linear MVP · Economy · Level Editor · Production
OVERVIEW

Magicaland Adventure evolved from a production-tested MVP that explored how Coin Master's Expedition model could move beyond a primarily linear progression structure. Based on Product, Business, and Economy requirements, the MVP introduced a controlled Main Road with optional Side Roads - creating space for bonus progression, risk–reward decisions, and deeper future product capabilities.

As a Product Level Designer, I translated this direction into playable layouts, Economy-informed configurations, content and asset requirements, level-building tool requirements, and a production-testable experience. The MVP became a direct foundation for Magicaland Adventure, which continued to evolve through multiple versions and supporting systems.

CHALLENGE

The existing Expedition model relied on a primarily linear progression structure. This made progression easy to understand and control, but limited optional depth, player agency, and the number of product levers available within the experience.

The opportunity was to explore whether a controlled non-linear structure could introduce meaningful Side Roads, bonus progression, and risk–reward decisions while preserving a clear Main Road - without investing prematurely in a dedicated Economy, Monetization layer, and new Chapter structures before the direction had been validated with real players. The challenge: build enough of the future experience to test it credibly in production, while keeping the MVP constrained, feasible, and compatible with existing systems.

ROLE & SCOPE

I translated Product, Business, and Economy direction into a production-testable gameplay experience.

  • ·Contributed to concept development, gameplay alternatives, and strategic direction.
  • ·Designed Main Road and optional Side Road layouts.
  • ·Translated Economy requirements, models, and simulation outputs into playable configurations, iterating on resource distribution, progression parameters, and route structure.
  • ·Created mockups, Chapter Dressing, and visual alternatives; planned existing, new, unique, and temporary MVP assets.
  • ·Coordinated content needs across Art, Animation, Tech Art, and Development.
  • ·Defined Level Builder requirements for the non-linear structure and supported configuration export & integration.
  • ·Clarified critical gameplay and configuration scenarios for QA and Operations, and participated in shared triage.

MAIN ROAD &
BONUS ROUTES

The MVP introduced a clear Main Road alongside optional Bonus Routes. The Main Road preserved the primary journey and Stage completion path, while Bonus Routes created additional decisions around cost, reward, timing, and opportunity cost.

  • ·Partial information - the route and reward destination were visible, but exact cost and full reward value were not always exposed in advance, preserving curiosity and discovery.
  • ·Backtracking - players could return along a cleared path; previously cleared obstacles stayed open, with no repeated Potion cost.
  • ·Optional by design - Bonus Routes were not required to complete the Stage, but could offer meaningful value inside the event and across the wider product.

EXECUTION &
COLLABORATION
Building the First Version

Turning an early concept into an executable experience required reducing a broad and still-evolving direction into a smaller set of connected systems that could be built, tested, and refined incrementally.

I worked across Product, Economy, Art, Development, and internal-tool stakeholders to define the initial progression structure, clarify the relationships between event actions and core-game value, and specify the supporting features needed to make the experience understandable and operationally manageable.

The economy was not a fixed system handed over for implementation. I worked alongside Product and Economy as assumptions around resource costs, rewards, progression, and player choice were translated into playable flows. Building the experience made it possible to evaluate those assumptions in context, identify friction or unclear value, and refine the system together.

Rather than documenting every part of the experience at the same level of detail, I focused specifications on the highest-friction areas: decisions that affected player understanding, progression, implementation, operational needs, or future scalability. I also wrote PRDs for supporting features and internal tools where clearer functional requirements were needed.

Migrating from Linear to Non-Linear

The migration from a linear to a non-linear model was designed as a gradual strategic process rather than a complete redesign. The initial goal was to recreate maps that remained as close as possible to the proven linear experience while running on the new non-linear infrastructure. This reduced the number of variables changing at once and created a clearer baseline for understanding player behavior.

I helped translate this strategy into player-facing layouts and flows, coordinating requirements and dependencies across stakeholders and supporting the product and business rationale behind the transition. Additional assumptions were introduced progressively, based on tests conducted by the Strategy team — preserve what was working, expose selected new capabilities, evaluate the results, and expand or revise based on evidence.

Growing Beyond the MVP

Following the MVP, Magicaland continued to evolve through strategic pivots, new player-facing features, and an expanding set of internal tools and production capabilities. My scope grew with the product. I owned specific features, flows, layouts, requirements, and internal-tool processes, taking them from early exploration and stakeholder alignment through implementation and iteration.

As different areas of the product became more interconnected, the work required close coordination with adjacent owners and cross-functional stakeholders. The challenge was not only to deliver individual features, but to evolve them within a growing system without creating contradictions across progression, infrastructure, operational needs, or player value.

OUTCOME
  • ·The MVP was tested in production and became a direct foundation for Magicaland Adventure.
  • ·The experience continued through multiple versions and additional capabilities.
  • ·Established a repeatable progression and production model beyond a single feature concept.
REFLECTION
What Changed in My Product Thinking

Magicaland changed how I think about product development in evolving systems. The first challenge was not to define the complete future state, but to identify a version that was coherent enough to build, test, and learn from — separating what had to be resolved for the MVP from decisions that could remain flexible until stronger evidence emerged.

As the product evolved, strategy, economy, player experience, infrastructure, and operational tools continuously shaped one another. A change in one area rarely remained local. It could affect progression, player choice, production workflows, technical requirements, and the value exchanged between the event and the core game.

The migration from a linear to a non-linear model reinforced the value of controlled change. Recreating a familiar experience on the new infrastructure first allowed the team to reduce the number of variables changing at once, establish a clearer baseline, and introduce new assumptions gradually through testing.

This showed me that transformation does not always require changing the player experience and the underlying system at the same time. Preserving familiarity can be a deliberate product strategy that enables clearer learning and lowers implementation and adoption risk.

The main lesson I carried forward: product clarity does not come from defining everything upfront. It comes from structuring change in a way that generates useful evidence, reduces avoidable risk, and allows each iteration to improve both the player experience and the system behind it.
